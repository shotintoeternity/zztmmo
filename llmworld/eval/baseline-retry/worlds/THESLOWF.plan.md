## Grounding notes

- Grief/visitation dreams of a lost loved one are a genuinely common bereavement experience; researcher Dr. Joshua Black is the field's most-cited voice. <cite index="53-0">Dr. Joshua Black's research highlights that grief dreams are a very common experience for the bereaved.</cite> Honor this by making the dream feel personal, not spooky.
- A recurring, oddly specific dream motif: the dead speak by telephone. <cite index="7-1">In these conceptual conversations, the deceased is very likely to be communicating with the dreamer via telephone.</cite> The phonebooth board should lean into a floating booth and a fading voice.
- Emotional arc should trend toward comfort, not horror. <cite index="54-0">Negative dreams tend to occur earlier in grief or when trauma is unresolved; as you process your grief, your dreams are likely to become more positive and comforting.</cite> Let the endgame land as bittersweet acceptance rather than terror.
- The "slow forgetting" is real memory science: the Ebbinghaus forgetting curve. <cite index="42-1,42-2">The Ebbinghaus forgetting curve shows the rate at which human memory deteriorates over time, characterized by rapid memory decline within 24 hours, followed by a slower rate.</cite> Stage boards so detail/color/sound erode gradually across the route.
- Memory decay is exponential, not linear — sharp at first, then a long thinning tail. <cite index="40-0">Ebbinghaus established that memory retention follows an exponential decay pattern over time, with memory traces weakening rapidly in the initial period.</cite> Early boards stay vivid; later boards go sparse and washed-out.
- Core mechanism to dramatize: without reinforcement, recall keeps slipping. <cite index="44-0">If we learn something new but make no attempt to relearn it, we remember less and less of it as the hours, days, and weeks go by.</cite> The facewall recall puzzle is the one moment the dreamer tries, and can, to hold on.
- Iconic dream-visitation imagery to reuse sparingly and high-contrast: a face among clouds, angels, a floating phone booth. <cite index="6-0">A dreamer described seeing a face in a phone booth floating among the clouds, with three angels flying around.</cite>
- Tone/palette: soft blues and grays draining to near-monochrome; warm lamplight only at the start (bedside) and its absence at the end (emptyroom). Two boards are dark (phonebooth, foglake) to physically enact vision — and memory — narrowing.
